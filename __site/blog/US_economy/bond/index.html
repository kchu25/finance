Introduction to Bonds and US Treasuries

What Are Bonds?

Bonds are debt securities that represent a loan made by an investor to a borrower (typically a corporation or government). When you buy a bond, you're essentially lending money in exchange for periodic interest payments and the return of the bond's face value at maturity.

Key Bond Concepts:

  • Face Value (Par Value): The amount the bond will be worth at maturity, typically $1,000

  • Coupon Rate: The interest rate the bond pays annually

  • Maturity Date: When the principal is repaid to the bondholder

  • Yield: The actual return an investor receives, which can differ from the coupon rate based on the purchase price

The Bond Market

The bond market is where debt securities are issued and traded. It's one of the largest financial markets globally, often larger than the stock market. Bonds can be bought and sold on the primary market (new issues) or secondary market (existing bonds).

US Treasuries

US Treasuries are debt securities issued by the US Department of the Treasury on behalf of the federal government. They're considered one of the safest investments in the world because they're backed by the "full faith and credit" of the US government.

Types of US Treasuries:

  1. Treasury Bills (T-Bills): Short-term securities that mature in one year or less (4, 8, 13, 26, or 52 weeks). Sold at a discount and don't pay interest before maturity.

  2. Treasury Notes (T-Notes): Medium-term securities with maturities of 2, 3, 5, 7, or 10 years. Pay interest every six months.

  3. Treasury Bonds (T-Bonds): Long-term securities with maturities of 20 or 30 years. Pay interest every six months.

  4. Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS): Securities whose principal adjusts with inflation as measured by the Consumer Price Index.

Why US Treasuries Matter

  • Benchmark Rates: Treasury yields serve as benchmarks for many other interest rates in the economy, including mortgages and corporate bonds

  • Safe Haven: Investors flock to Treasuries during economic uncertainty

  • Monetary Policy: The Federal Reserve uses Treasury securities to implement monetary policy

  • Government Financing: Treasuries fund government operations and deficit spending

Bond Prices and Yields

Bond prices and yields move inversely. When interest rates rise, existing bond prices fall (because newer bonds offer higher rates). When interest rates fall, existing bond prices rise. This relationship is fundamental to understanding bond market dynamics.

The Federal Reserve and Interest Rates

The Federal Reserve is deeply connected to the Treasury market and plays a crucial role in controlling interest rates throughout the economy.

The Fed Funds Rate:

The Federal Reserve's primary tool is the federal funds rate — the interest rate at which banks lend to each other overnight. When people say "the Fed raised rates" or "the Fed cut rates," they're referring to this rate.

Why do banks lend to each other? Banks are required to maintain a certain level of reserves (cash) at the Federal Reserve each night. Sometimes a bank ends the day with excess reserves, while another bank might fall short. Rather than holding idle cash or facing penalties, banks with excess reserves lend to banks that need them overnight. This interbank lending market helps the banking system run smoothly. The interest rate on these overnight loans is the federal funds rate.

How the Fed Controls Rates:

  1. Open Market Operations: The Fed buys and sells US Treasury securities in the open market. When it buys Treasuries, it injects money into the banking system, lowering interest rates. When it sells Treasuries, it removes money from the system, raising rates.

  2. Interest on Reserve Balances: The Fed pays interest on the reserves that banks hold at the Fed, which influences how much banks are willing to lend.

  3. Discount Window: The Fed lends directly to banks at the "discount rate," which affects overall lending rates.

The Connection to Treasuries:

  • Yield Curve Impact: When the Fed changes the fed funds rate, it most directly affects short-term rates, including short-term Treasury bills. Longer-term Treasury yields (like 10-year or 30-year bonds) are influenced by market expectations of future Fed policy and inflation.

  • Quantitative Easing (QE): During economic crises, the Fed may buy large quantities of Treasuries (and other securities) to lower long-term interest rates and stimulate the economy.

How does buying Treasuries lower rates? When the Fed buys Treasuries, it increases demand for these bonds. Higher demand drives up bond prices. Since bond prices and yields move inversely, when prices go up, yields (interest rates) go down. Additionally, the Fed creates new money to buy these bonds, injecting liquidity into the financial system and encouraging lending and investment.

Where does the Fed get the money? Yes, essentially they "print" it digitally! The Fed doesn't need to have money on hand — it simply credits the bank accounts of Treasury sellers with new electronic reserves. It's not physically printing cash, but electronically creating money with accounting entries. This is one of the Fed's unique powers as a central bank. When they buy $1 billion in Treasuries, they literally create $1 billion in new bank reserves out of thin air. This is why QE is sometimes called "printing money," though it's more accurately creating digital money in the banking system.

So they just "beep" and buy Treasuries? Pretty much, yes! The Fed announces they want to buy Treasuries, sellers come forward, and the Fed simply updates electronic ledgers to credit the sellers' accounts with newly created money. No existing funds needed, no taxes collected, no borrowing required — just a digital entry that says "you now have $X billion more in your account." It's one of the most extraordinary powers in the financial system, and it only works because the Fed is the central bank with the authority to create money.

Wait, does this "beep" increase the money supply or add to the debt? Great question! It increases the money supply, NOT the national debt. Here's the key distinction: When the government issues new Treasuries to fund spending, that adds to the national debt. But when the Fed buys existing Treasuries (in QE), it's just swapping one asset (Treasuries) for another (cash/bank reserves) in the financial system. The Fed creates new money and puts it into circulation, expanding the money supply. The debt itself stays the same — the Treasury bonds still exist, they've just moved from private hands to the Fed's balance sheet. This is why some people worry QE could cause inflation: more money chasing the same amount of goods and services.

  • Quantitative Tightening (QT): The opposite of QE — the Fed allows its holdings of Treasuries to mature without replacing them, which can push rates higher.

Why This Matters:

The Fed's actions ripple through the entire financial system. When the Fed raises rates, borrowing becomes more expensive for everyone — from consumers taking out mortgages to corporations issuing bonds. When it lowers rates, borrowing becomes cheaper, stimulating economic activity. Treasury yields serve as the foundation for virtually all other interest rates in the economy, which is why Fed policy and the Treasury market are so closely intertwined.

How Bank Loans Connect to Treasuries

Are bank loans related to bonds and Treasuries? Absolutely! Treasury yields serve as the baseline "risk-free" rate for the entire financial system. When banks decide what interest rate to charge you for a mortgage, car loan, or business loan, they start with Treasury yields and add a premium on top based on risk.

The Connection Works Like This:

  • Treasury yields = Risk-free baseline: Since US Treasuries are considered the safest investment (backed by the government), their yields represent the minimum return investors expect.

  • Bank loans = Treasuries + Risk premium: Banks look at Treasury yields and add extra interest to cover the risk that you might not repay. A mortgage might be "10-year Treasury yield + 2%". A business loan might be "5-year Treasury + 3-5%" depending on the business's creditworthiness.

Do banks actually use Treasuries to make loans? No, not directly. Banks don't need to own Treasuries to make you a loan. When a bank gives you a mortgage or business loan, they're creating that money (similar to how the Fed does, but on a smaller scale through fractional reserve banking). The connection to Treasuries is about pricing, not funding. Treasuries serve as the benchmark rate that determines what interest rate makes sense. However, banks often DO own large amounts of Treasuries as investments to keep their money safe and earn returns. But those Treasury holdings are separate from the loan-making process itself.

  • When Treasuries rise, loans get more expensive: If the 10-year Treasury yield goes from 3% to 5%, mortgage rates will also increase by roughly the same amount. If Treasuries fall, loan rates generally fall too.

Example: If the 10-year Treasury yields 4% and banks typically add 2% for mortgage risk, your mortgage rate would be around 6%. If the Treasury yield jumps to 5%, your mortgage rate would rise to around 7%.

Are Bonds and US Treasuries Different or the Same?

They're related but not the same thing:

  • "Bonds" is the general category: A bond is any debt security where you lend money and receive interest. This includes corporate bonds, municipal bonds, US Treasuries, foreign government bonds, and more.

  • "US Treasuries" are a specific type of bond: They're bonds issued specifically by the US federal government. So all US Treasuries are bonds, but not all bonds are US Treasuries.

Think of it like this: "Vehicle" is the general category, while "Toyota Camry" is a specific type of vehicle. Similarly, "bond" is the general category, while "US Treasury" is a specific type of bond.

Other Types of Bonds:

  • Corporate bonds: Issued by companies like Apple or Ford

  • Municipal bonds: Issued by state and local governments

  • Agency bonds: Issued by government agencies like Fannie Mae

  • International bonds: Issued by foreign governments or companies

Each type has different risk levels, yields, and tax treatments, but they all follow the same basic principle: you lend money, receive interest, and get your principal back at maturity.
