Optimal Stopping: A Discrete Approach

Why Work in Log Space?

When trading stocks, a $1 move means very different things for a $10 stock versus a $1000 stock. Log prices normalize this perfectly.

The magic of logarithms: If you buy at price P0P_0P0​ and sell at P1P_1P1​, your log return is:

ln⁡(P1)−ln⁡(P0)=ln⁡(P1P0)\ln(P_1) - \ln(P_0) = \ln\left(\frac{P_1}{P_0}\right)ln(P1​)−ln(P0​)=ln(P0​P1​​)

This is exactly your percentage return! A 10% gain gives ln⁡(1.10)≈0.0953\ln(1.10) \approx 0.0953ln(1.10)≈0.0953, and a 10% loss gives ln⁡(0.90)≈−0.1054\ln(0.90) \approx -0.1054ln(0.90)≈−0.1054. Notice these appear symmetric in regular space (both "10%"), but they're actually asymmetric everywhere—including log space! The real benefit of log space is that it captures the true asymmetry of compounding.

Mean Reversion in Discrete Time

Think of mean reversion like a rubber band. When a stock's log price xtx_txt​ drifts away from its long-run average μ\muμ, there's a pull bringing it back.

Mean reversion: The tendency for prices to return to their historical average over time. Like gravity for stock prices.

In discrete time steps (say, every minute or every hour), the log price updates like this:

xt+1=xt+θ(μ−xt)Δt+σΔt⋅εtx_{t+1} = x_t + \theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta t + \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t} \cdot \varepsilon_txt+1​=xt​+θ(μ−xt​)Δt+σΔt​⋅εt​

Where:

  • xt=ln⁡(Pt)x_t = \ln(P_t)xt​=ln(Pt​) is the log price at time ttt (unitless, it's a logarithm)

  • θ\thetaθ = reversion speed (units: time−1\text{time}^{-1}time−1, e.g., "per hour")

  • μ\muμ = long-run log price average (unitless)

  • σ\sigmaσ = volatility (units: time−1/2\text{time}^{-1/2}time−1/2, e.g., "per hour\sqrt{\text{hour}}hour​")

  • Δt\Delta tΔt = time step size (e.g., 0.1 hours = 6 minutes)

  • εt∼N(0,1)\varepsilon_t \sim N(0,1)εt​∼N(0,1) = random shock (unitless)

Units and interpretation: All time must be in the same units! If θ=2.0\theta = 2.0θ=2.0 per hour and σ=0.15\sigma = 0.15σ=0.15 per hour\sqrt{\text{hour}}hour​, then Δt\Delta tΔt must be in hours (not minutes).

What does θ=2.0\theta = 2.0θ=2.0 mean? The "half-life" of deviations is ln⁡(2)/θ≈0.35\ln(2)/\theta \approx 0.35ln(2)/θ≈0.35 hours (~20 minutes). After 20 minutes, half of any price deviation from μ\muμ gets corrected. Larger θ\thetaθ means faster snap-back.

Why the weird units? θ⋅Δt\theta \cdot \Delta tθ⋅Δt needs to be unitless (it's multiplied by the unitless quantity μ−xt\mu - x_tμ−xt​). Similarly, σΔt\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}σΔt​ must be unitless to match log price changes.

Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) process: A fancy name for mean-reverting random walk. The continuous version of what we're showing here.

Key insight: The drift term θ(μ−xt)Δt\theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta tθ(μ−xt​)Δt is positive when xt<μx_t < \muxt​<μ (below average → pushes up) and negative when xt>μx_t > \muxt​>μ (above average → pushes down).

The Decision Rule

You entered a trade at log price xentryx_{\text{entry}}xentry​. Now at time ttt, you're at xtx_txt​. Should you exit?

Current profit:

Profitnow=xt−xentry\text{Profit}_{\text{now}} = x_t - x_{\text{entry}}Profitnow​=xt​−xentry​

Expected profit if you wait one more time step:

First, compute the expected log price:

E[xt+1∣xt]=xt+θ(μ−xt)Δt\mathbb{E}[x_{t+1} | x_t] = x_t + \theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta tE[xt+1​∣xt​]=xt​+θ(μ−xt​)Δt

Why is this the expectation? Let's go slow. Starting from our update rule:

xt+1=xt+θ(μ−xt)Δt+σΔt⋅εtx_{t+1} = x_t + \theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta t + \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t} \cdot \varepsilon_txt+1​=xt​+θ(μ−xt​)Δt+σΔt​⋅εt​

We want to find: "What do I expect xt+1x_{t+1}xt+1​ to be, given that I know xtx_txt​ right now?"

Step 1: Take the conditional expectation of both sides (conditioning on knowing xtx_txt​):

E[xt+1∣xt]=E[xt+θ(μ−xt)Δt+σΔt⋅εt ∣ xt]\mathbb{E}[x_{t+1} | x_t] = \mathbb{E}\left[x_t + \theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta t + \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t} \cdot \varepsilon_t \,\big|\, x_t\right]E[xt+1​∣xt​]=E[xt​+θ(μ−xt​)Δt+σΔt​⋅εt​∣∣​xt​]

Step 2: Split up the expectation (linearity):

E[xt+1∣xt]=E[xt∣xt]+E[θ(μ−xt)Δt∣xt]+E[σΔt⋅εt∣xt]\mathbb{E}[x_{t+1} | x_t] = \mathbb{E}[x_t | x_t] + \mathbb{E}[\theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta t | x_t] + \mathbb{E}[\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t} \cdot \varepsilon_t | x_t]E[xt+1​∣xt​]=E[xt​∣xt​]+E[θ(μ−xt​)Δt∣xt​]+E[σΔt​⋅εt​∣xt​]

Step 3: Simplify each term:

  • E[xt∣xt]=xt\mathbb{E}[x_t | x_t] = x_tE[xt​∣xt​]=xt​ (if I know xtx_txt​, its expected value is just itself)

  • E[θ(μ−xt)Δt∣xt]=θ(μ−xt)Δt\mathbb{E}[\theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta t | x_t] = \theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta tE[θ(μ−xt​)Δt∣xt​]=θ(μ−xt​)Δt (everything here is known/deterministic given xtx_txt​)

  • E[σΔt⋅εt∣xt]=σΔt⋅E[εt∣xt]=σΔt⋅0=0\mathbb{E}[\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t} \cdot \varepsilon_t | x_t] = \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t} \cdot \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_t | x_t] = \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t} \cdot 0 = 0E[σΔt​⋅εt​∣xt​]=σΔt​⋅E[εt​∣xt​]=σΔt​⋅0=0

(The last one is zero because εt\varepsilon_tεt​ is independent of xtx_txt​ and has mean zero)

Step 4: Put it together:

E[xt+1∣xt]=xt+θ(μ−xt)Δt\mathbb{E}[x_{t+1} | x_t] = x_t + \theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta tE[xt+1​∣xt​]=xt​+θ(μ−xt​)Δt

Intuition: On average, the random shocks cancel out. Only the deterministic drift (mean reversion pull) remains.

Then the expected profit is:

Profitexpected=E[xt+1∣xt]−xentry\text{Profit}_{\text{expected}} = \mathbb{E}[x_{t+1} | x_t] - x_{\text{entry}}Profitexpected​=E[xt+1​∣xt​]−xentry​

Exit rule: Exit if: xt−xentry≥E[xt+1∣xt]−xentry−c\text{Exit if: } x_t - x_{\text{entry}} \geq \mathbb{E}[x_{t+1} | x_t] - x_{\text{entry}} - cExit if: xt​−xentry​≥E[xt+1​∣xt​]−xentry​−c

Intuition behind the exit rule: This compares two options:

  • Left side (xt−xentryx_t - x_{\text{entry}}xt​−xentry​): Profit if you exit now

  • Right side (E[xt+1∣xt]−xentry−c\mathbb{E}[x_{t+1} | x_t] - x_{\text{entry}} - cE[xt+1​∣xt​]−xentry​−c): Expected profit if you wait one more step, minus holding costs

Exit when the left ≥ right, meaning "taking profit now is at least as good as waiting."

Three scenarios:

  1. You're in profit and far from equilibrium: Expected future profit > current profit → keep holding (mean reversion will push you higher)

  2. You're in profit and near equilibrium: Expected future profit ≈ current profit → exit (not much more to gain, why pay holding costs?)

  3. Expected future is negative: You expect things to get worse → exit immediately, regardless of current position

The beauty: this single rule handles taking profits, cutting losses, and everything in between!

Where ccc is your holding cost (trading fees, slippage, opportunity cost).

Simplifying:

Exit if: xt≥xt+θ(μ−xt)Δt−c\text{Exit if: } x_t \geq x_t + \theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta t - cExit if: xt​≥xt​+θ(μ−xt​)Δt−c Exit if: 0≥θ(μ−xt)Δt−c\text{Exit if: } 0 \geq \theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta t - cExit if: 0≥θ(μ−xt​)Δt−c Exit if: θ(μ−xt)Δt≤c\text{Exit if: } \theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta t \leq cExit if: θ(μ−xt​)Δt≤c

Translation: Exit when the expected mean reversion pull is smaller than your holding costs.

Estimating Parameters from Historical Data

In practice, you don't know μ\muμ and θ\thetaθ in advance—you need to estimate them from past prices.

The approach: Use regression on historical log price changes. If you have log prices x0,x1,x2,…,xnx_0, x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_nx0​,x1​,x2​,…,xn​ at equal time intervals Δt\Delta tΔt, then:

xt+1−xt=θ(μ−xt)Δt+noisex_{t+1} - x_t = \theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta t + \text{noise}xt+1​−xt​=θ(μ−xt​)Δt+noise

Rearranging:

xt+1−xt=θμΔt−θΔt⋅xt+noisex_{t+1} - x_t = \theta\mu\Delta t - \theta\Delta t \cdot x_t + \text{noise}xt+1​−xt​=θμΔt−θΔt⋅xt​+noise

This is just linear regression! Let yt=xt+1−xty_t = x_{t+1} - x_tyt​=xt+1​−xt​ (the log price change) and run:

yt=β0+β1xt+errory_t = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_t + \text{error}yt​=β0​+β1​xt​+error

From the regression coefficients:

  • θ=−β1/Δt\theta = -\beta_1 / \Delta tθ=−β1​/Δt

  • μ=−β0/β1\mu = -\beta_0 / \beta_1μ=−β0​/β1​

  • σ=std(residuals)/Δt\sigma = \text{std(residuals)} / \sqrt{\Delta t}σ=std(residuals)/Δt​

How much history? More is better, but too much includes irrelevant regimes. A common choice: 20-60 days of minute-by-minute or hourly data for intraday trading. The tradeoff is between statistical precision (needs more data) and stationarity (parameters change over time).

The workflow with minute-by-minute data:

Step 1: Collect your minute-by-minute log prices: x0,x1,x2,…,xnx_0, x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_nx0​,x1​,x2​,…,xn​

Step 2: Run the regression:

  • Compute changes: yt=xt+1−xty_t = x_{t+1} - x_tyt​=xt+1​−xt​ (log price change from minute ttt to t+1t+1t+1)

  • Regress: yt=β0+β1xt+errory_t = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_t + \text{error}yt​=β0​+β1​xt​+error

  • Get coefficients: β0\beta_0β0​ and β1\beta_1β1​

Step 3: Convert to parameters. Now you choose your preferred time units:

Option A - Work in hours (most common):

  • Set Δt=1/60\Delta t = 1/60Δt=1/60 hours (your data points are 1 minute = 1/60 hour apart)

  • Calculate: θ=−β1/(1/60)=−60β1\theta = -\beta_1 / (1/60) = -60\beta_1θ=−β1​/(1/60)=−60β1​ per hour

  • Calculate: μ=−β0/β1\mu = -\beta_0 / \beta_1μ=−β0​/β1​ (unitless, same either way)

  • Calculate: σ=std(residuals)/1/60\sigma = \text{std(residuals)} / \sqrt{1/60}σ=std(residuals)/1/60​ per hour\sqrt{\text{hour}}hour​

Option B - Work in minutes:

  • Set Δt=1\Delta t = 1Δt=1 minute (your data points are 1 minute apart)

  • Calculate: θ=−β1/1=−β1\theta = -\beta_1 / 1 = -\beta_1θ=−β1​/1=−β1​ per minute

  • Calculate: μ=−β0/β1\mu = -\beta_0 / \beta_1μ=−β0​/β1​ (same)

  • Calculate: σ=std(residuals)/1\sigma = \text{std(residuals)} / 1σ=std(residuals)/1 per minute\sqrt{\text{minute}}minute​

Both give identical predictions! Example: if regression gives β1=−0.0333\beta_1 = -0.0333β1​=−0.0333, then θ=2.0\theta = 2.0θ=2.0 per hour is the same as θ=0.0333\theta = 0.0333θ=0.0333 per minute. Most people prefer hours because θ=2.0\theta = 2.0θ=2.0 per hour is easier to interpret than θ=0.0333\theta = 0.0333θ=0.0333 per minute.

The big assumption: You're assuming the mean-reverting dynamics you observed historically will continue today. If the market regime has changed (news, volatility spike, trend), your parameters could be wrong. This is the fundamental challenge of all statistical trading!

Stationarity requirement: This strategy assumes μ\muμ, θ\thetaθ, and σ\sigmaσ remain constant over time (stationarity). In reality, markets violate this constantly:

  • Regime shifts: Earnings announcement → new equilibrium μ\muμ

  • Volatility spikes: Market panic → σ\sigmaσ jumps dramatically

  • Trending periods: Sustained uptrend → mean reversion temporarily breaks down

  • Intraday patterns: Different dynamics at market open vs. close

What kind of stationarity? The OU process models xt=ln⁡(Pt)x_t = \ln(P_t)xt​=ln(Pt​) as mean-reverting around μ\muμ. This means:

  • xtx_txt​ itself is NOT stationary (it fluctuates around μ\muμ but the level matters)

  • However, (xt−μ)(x_t - \mu)(xt​−μ)IS stationary (deviations from mean are stationary)

  • The increments Δxt=xt+1−xt\Delta x_t = x_{t+1} - x_tΔxt​=xt+1​−xt​ are also stationary

The estimation-application gap: You estimate θ\thetaθ and μ\muμ from stationary differences/deviations in historical data, but then apply them to the current non-stationary price level xtx_txt​. Why does this work?

  • The OU model says: the drift θ(μ−xt)Δt\theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta tθ(μ−xt​)Δt pulls xtx_txt​ toward μ\muμ, regardless of the current level

  • Since (xt−μ)(x_t - \mu)(xt​−μ) is stationary, the dynamics are the same whether xt=4.50x_t = 4.50xt​=4.50 or xt=4.70x_t = 4.70xt​=4.70

  • Your estimated parameters capture these dynamics, which you assume will govern future price movements

The leap of faith: You're betting that mean-reverting behavior observed historically will continue to work at today's price level. This breaks when:

  • μ\muμ has shifted (regime change) → your historical estimate is outdated

  • Market conditions changed (volatility spike, trending) → dynamics no longer apply

  • Price breaks far beyond expected range → signal that parameters may be invalid

This is why traders constantly monitor whether current price behavior matches their estimated model!

Why the regression works: When you run: yt=xt+1−xt=β0+β1xt+errory_t = x_{t+1} - x_t = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_t + \text{error}yt​=xt+1​−xt​=β0​+β1​xt​+error

You're regressing differences (yty_tyt​) on levels (xtx_txt​). This is valid for mean-reverting processes but would be problematic for random walks! The key:

  • Random walk (non-stationary): xt+1=xt+εtx_{t+1} = x_t + \varepsilon_txt+1​=xt​+εt​ → no mean reversion, regressing differences on levels is spurious

  • Mean reversion (stationary around μ\muμ): xt+1=xt+θ(μ−xt)Δt+εtx_{t+1} = x_t + \theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta t + \varepsilon_txt+1​=xt​+θ(μ−xt​)Δt+εt​ → the level xtx_txt​ predicts future changes because of the pull toward μ\muμ

How to test if your stock is mean-reverting:

  • Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test on xtx_txt​: Rejects unit root? → Evidence of mean reversion

  • Check autocorrelation of (xt−xˉ)(x_t - \bar{x})(xt​−xˉ): Negative at lag 1? → Mean reversion signal

If ADF fails to reject or autocorrelation is near zero, you likely have a random walk, not mean reversion!

What practitioners do:

  • Use shorter lookback windows (20-30 days) to capture recent regime

  • Re-estimate frequently (daily or intraday) to adapt to changes

  • Add trend filters (only trade mean reversion when no strong trend detected)

  • Monitor for breakouts (if price moves far beyond expected range, pause trading)

The OU process is mathematically stationary around its mean, but real markets aren't. Managing this gap between model and reality is the art of statistical trading.

Quick sanity checks:

  • θ>0\theta > 0θ>0? (Must be positive for mean reversion)

  • μ\muμ near recent average log price? (If wildly different, model may not fit)

  • σ\sigmaσ reasonable? (Compare to historical volatility measures)

Why must θ>0\theta > 0θ>0? Consider the drift term θ(μ−xt)Δt\theta(\mu - x_t)\Delta tθ(μ−xt​)Δt:

  • If xt<μx_t < \muxt​<μ (below average): (μ−xt)>0(\mu - x_t) > 0(μ−xt​)>0

    • With θ>0\theta > 0θ>0: drift is positive → pushes price up toward μ\muμ ✓

    • With θ<0\theta < 0θ<0: drift is negative → pushes price down away from μ\muμ ✗

  • If xt>μx_t > \muxt​>μ (above average): (μ−xt)<0(\mu - x_t) < 0(μ−xt​)<0

    • With θ>0\theta > 0θ>0: drift is negative → pushes price down toward μ\muμ ✓

    • With θ<0\theta < 0θ<0: drift is positive → pushes price up away from μ\muμ ✗

If θ<0\theta < 0θ<0: You get explosive trending (away from μ\muμ), not mean reversion! If your regression gives β1>0\beta_1 > 0β1​>0 (so θ=−β1/Δt<0\theta = -\beta_1/\Delta t < 0θ=−β1​/Δt<0), the stock isn't mean-reverting—it's trending. The optimal stopping framework doesn't apply; you need a different strategy!

A Concrete Example

You're trading a stock that normally sits at μ=4.605\mu = 4.605μ=4.605 (about $100\$100$100). It's currently oversold at xt=4.50x_t = 4.50xt​=4.50 (about $90\$90$90).

Parameters:

  • Mean reversion speed: θ=2.0\theta = 2.0θ=2.0 per hour

  • Volatility: σ=0.15\sigma = 0.15σ=0.15 per hour

  • Time step: Δt=0.1\Delta t = 0.1Δt=0.1 hours (6 minutes)

  • Holding cost: c=0.001c = 0.001c=0.001 (0.1%)

Expected log price in 6 minutes:

E[xt+1]=4.50+2.0×(4.605−4.50)×0.1=4.521\mathbb{E}[x_{t+1}] = 4.50 + 2.0 \times (4.605 - 4.50) \times 0.1 = 4.521E[xt+1​]=4.50+2.0×(4.605−4.50)×0.1=4.521

In dollar terms: e4.521≈$91.90e^{4.521} \approx \$91.90e4.521≈$91.90

Your decision:

  • Current profit: 4.50−4.50=04.50 - 4.50 = 04.50−4.50=0 log points

  • Expected profit: 4.521−4.50=0.0214.521 - 4.50 = 0.0214.521−4.50=0.021 log points ≈ 2.1%

  • Since 0.021>0.0010.021 > 0.0010.021>0.001, keep holding! The expected gain exceeds holding costs.

Why Log Space Matters for Stops and Targets

Many traders set symmetric percentage stops in price space. This is a mistake!

Example: Stock at $100, trader sets:

  • Target: $110 (+10%)

  • Stop: $90 (−10%)

In log space:

  • Target: ln⁡(110)−ln⁡(100)=0.0953\ln(110) - \ln(100) = 0.0953ln(110)−ln(100)=0.0953

  • Stop: ln⁡(90)−ln⁡(100)=−0.1054\ln(90) - \ln(100) = -0.1054ln(90)−ln(100)=−0.1054

Asymmetric! You're risking 10.54% to gain 9.53%.

Corrected symmetric setup in log space:

  • Target: ln⁡(100)+0.10=4.705\ln(100) + 0.10 = 4.705ln(100)+0.10=4.705 → $110.52\$110.52$110.52

  • Stop: ln⁡(100)−0.10=4.505\ln(100) - 0.10 = 4.505ln(100)−0.10=4.505 → $90.48\$90.48$90.48

Now risk/reward is truly balanced.

The Big Picture

Optimal stopping with log prices gives you:

  1. Normalization: A 1% move is a 1% move, regardless of whether the stock is $10 or $1000

  2. Linear dynamics: Mean reversion becomes a simple linear pull in log space

  3. Stable probabilities: The chance of moving up 0.1 log points is the same everywhere

  4. Correct risk assessment: Stops and targets are properly symmetric

The decision rule is beautifully simple: exit when the mean reversion pull becomes weaker than your costs.

Further Notes

Kelly Criterion: A formula for optimal bet sizing that maximizes log wealth. It naturally lives in log space too, which is why all these pieces fit together so nicely.

Scaled exits: Even with optimal stopping, many traders benefit from scaling out of positions (exiting in chunks). This reduces variance in your returns, which compounds to better long-term growth.
